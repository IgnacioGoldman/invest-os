# Macro Theme Research

Role:

Use this skill when portfolio recommendations need to identify themes, sectors,
verticals, or geographies that may fit the current macroeconomic environment.

Core workflow:

1. Research the current macro backdrop before recommending themes.
2. Prefer recent, durable sources:
   - IMF World Economic Outlook
   - OECD Economic Outlook
   - World Bank Global Economic Prospects
   - IEA energy outlook/investment reports
   - Central-bank or regulator commentary when rates, inflation, or liquidity
     are central to the recommendation
   - Fund issuer or company primary sources for investable exposure mapping
3. Translate macro conditions into portfolio-relevant opportunity areas.
4. Compare those opportunity areas against the current portfolio's existing
   exposure.
5. Recommend research directions or staged deployment areas, not guaranteed
   trades.

Macro-to-portfolio lenses:

- Growth and productivity:
  - AI infrastructure, automation, semiconductors, cloud, cybersecurity, and
    enterprise software may benefit from productivity investment.
  - If the portfolio is already concentrated in technology, treat these as
    existing exposure rather than an automatic add.

- Inflation, energy, and geopolitics:
  - Energy security, power grids, utilities, nuclear, storage, efficiency,
    industrial infrastructure, and commodities can become more relevant when
    energy shocks or geopolitical risks are elevated.
  - Prefer diversified or infrastructure-like exposure when the investor already
    holds high-volatility crypto or single-name technology.

- Rates and balance-sheet stress:
  - High-quality companies, profitable cash-generative businesses, short-duration
    fixed income, and defensive sectors can matter when yields, refinancing risk,
    or fiscal pressure are prominent.

- Demographics and resilience:
  - Healthcare, medical technology, insurance, staples, and other resilient
    demand areas can diversify technology-heavy portfolios.

- Geography:
  - Identify whether exposure is mostly US, developed markets, Europe, emerging
    markets, or single-country.
  - Broad global ETFs may already provide geography exposure, but they may still
    be market-cap weighted toward the US and large technology.
  - Consider whether incremental exposure should be US quality, Europe, Japan,
    emerging markets, or global ex-US depending on the current macro backdrop
    and the existing portfolio.

Output requirements:

- Name specific underrepresented themes, sectors, verticals, and geographies.
- State whether each is:
  - already represented
  - partially represented
  - missing or underrepresented
  - blocked by data quality
- Tie each suggestion to current macro evidence and portfolio evidence.
- Avoid generic phrases such as "consider diversification" without naming the
  diversification target.
- Avoid recommending additional exposure to a theme that is already a major
  concentration unless the action is explicitly a risk-managed continuation.

Example framing:

- "AI infrastructure is already indirectly represented through technology and
  MSTR/crypto-adjacent exposure; incremental capital should first research
  healthcare, energy infrastructure, or non-US quality exposure."
- "Energy-security and grid themes fit the macro backdrop, but use diversified
  vehicles or watchlist research before adding single-name risk."
- "Global ETFs provide broad geographic exposure, but the portfolio still lacks
  an explicit non-US or emerging-market thesis."
